Chapter 1 · Context & Vision

Skala Kallonis: A Tourism Hotspot in an Ecological Crown

Located at the heart of Lesvos, Skala Kallonis sits beside one of Europe's most important wetlands. Its booming summer tourism generates a hidden cost: organic food waste that currently goes unrecovered.

🐟

The Sardine Economy

World-renowned "Papalina" sardine festivals each August draw thousands of visitors, generating concentrated fish-waste streams from ~25 waterfront tavernas.

♨️

Tourism Pressure

~2.28 kg

food waste per guest per day generated by hospitality operations (international benchmark, tourism islands).

🦩

Natura 2000 Zone

The Gulf of Kalloni wetland hosts 311 bird species including flamingos. Any energy solution must safeguard this ecological asset.

⚡

Energy Isolation

As a Greek island, Lesvos relies on expensive grid imports. Energy self-sufficiency is both economically and strategically vital.

Why Biogas? Anaerobic digestion of food waste produces ~261 litres of biogas per kg of cooked food waste. Run through a CHP unit, 1 m³ of biogas yields 1.2–1.8 kWh of electricity — sufficient to power a household's daily lighting and basic appliances.

System Design: From Waste to Watts

A community-scale anaerobic digestion plant in the 50–100 m³ digester volume class is well-matched to Skala Kallonis's feedstock profile and serves 30 households plus the women's cooperative.

Recommended System Specification

Covered lagoon / CSTR (Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor) — 60–80 m³ working volume. Mesophilic operation at 35–38°C, enabled by waste heat from CHP unit. Hydraulic Retention Time (HRT): 20–30 days. Handles mixed substrates including fish waste (high N content balanced by carbohydrate-rich kitchen waste). A double-digester configuration (2 × 40 m³) allows maintenance without system shutdown.
Low-pressure gas bag storage (10–20 m³). H₂S removal via iron scrubber (essential for engine longevity). CO₂ remains — no upgrading required for CHP. Moisture separation via condensate trap. Safety pressure release valve. Estimated gas pressure: 10–20 mbar.
Modified internal combustion generator, 1–3 kW electrical output, running on 100% biogas. At 20–30% electrical efficiency: 1 m³ biogas → 1.2–1.8 kWh electricity. With 48,200 m³/year feedstock potential and conservative 60% utilisation: ~34,000–52,000 kWh/year electrical generation. Heat recovery from cooling water and exhaust feeds digester heating (winter) and cooperative hot water needs.
Approximately 70–80% of feedstock becomes liquid digestate — a nitrogen-rich biofertiliser. Estimated output: 130–170 tons/year. Applied to Kalloni valley olive orchards and vegetable plots. Eliminates chemical fertiliser cost (~€80–120/ton equivalent). This secondary revenue stream significantly improves project economics.
Under Greek Law 5037/2023 (REC framework) and Law 5215/2025 (biomethane support), RECs can apply virtual net metering (VNM) — surplus electricity credited against member bills. Connection to DEDDIE (island distribution network). Installed capacity likely <500 kW, falling under simplified licensing regime. Application via RAE (Regulatory Authority for Energy).
Climate Advantage Lesvos's Mediterranean climate (average 18°C annual) reduces digester heating requirements relative to northern Europe, improving year-round biogas yield consistency.

Chapter 5 · Transportation Feasibility

Collection Logistics & Waste Supply Chain

The compact geography of Skala Kallonis — a linear harbour village of roughly 1.5 km — makes waste collection extremely efficient. All major waste generators are within a single route.

🚛

Collection Route Design

Single daily electric-assist cargo vehicle (≤3.5 ton) with refrigerated compartment for fish waste. Total route: ~4 km round trip. Collection windows: 06:00–08:00 (restaurants) and 09:00–10:00 (households). Estimated fuel/energy cost: €3–5/day.

📦

Collection Infrastructure

25-litre HDPE bio-bins at each taverna. 120-litre shared bins per 3–4 households. Sealed to prevent odour and bird disturbance (flamingo habitat concern). Women's cooperative manages bin logistics as paid service (job creation).

📍

Collection Points Map

All 25 waterfront tavernas are within a 1.2 km linear stretch. 30 households clustered within 400m of digester Zone A. Zero inter-village transport needed for initial community.

🌿

Digestate Distribution

Liquid digestate returned to Kalloni valley agricultural plots via tractor-trailer (existing farm vehicles). Road to agricultural plain already tarmacked. Digestate sold to local farmers at €15–25/m³ as biofertiliser.

Seasonal Challenge & Mitigation

Winter Gap (Nov–Mar) Restaurant waste drops ~85% in off-season. Mitigation: co-digest with livestock manure from Kalloni valley farms (cattle, sheep — abundant locally), and olive mill wastewater (November crush season). Blending maintains digester stability through winter.

Chapter 7 · The Energy Community

Building the Cooperative: 30 Households + Women's Power

Under Greek Law 5037/2023, a Renewable Energy Community (REC) cooperative needs a minimum of 30 members — exactly our founding group. The women's cooperative as co-anchor adds economic resilience and social capital.

👩‍🌾

Women's Cooperative Role

Operates waste collection logistics (paid work). Uses CHP waste heat for olive oil processing, preserves production and food drying. Manages digestate sales to farmers. A minimum of 6 paid FTE equivalent positions created.

🏠

30 Founding Households

Each member contributes organic kitchen waste, receives electricity bill credits (~€40–60/month), and holds one vote in the cooperative's general assembly. Share capital: €500–1,000 per member.

🍽️

Taverna Partners

Restaurants participate as waste-supply partners (not full members unless desired). They pay a tipping fee instead of skip hire, gaining marketing value as "circular economy restaurants" — a growing tourism differentiator.

🏛️

Municipal Partnership

Municipality of Western Lesvos can participate as a member under Law 5037/2023, providing land (Zone A), political support, and access to ESPA/EU grant applications. Municipality also benefits from reduced waste management costs.

Governance Structure

General Assembly (all 30+ members)

Votes on major decisions. One member, one vote. Annual meeting + extraordinary sessions. Surplus distribution policy set here.

Board of Directors (5 members)

Elected triannually. Includes at least 2 women's cooperative representatives. Manages daily operations, contracts, and financial oversight.

Technical Manager (paid, 0.5 FTE)

Monitors digester operation, liaisons with DEDDIE, handles permits and environmental compliance reporting.

Cooperative Logistics Team (3–4 FTE)

Waste collection, digestate distribution, and food processing (women's coop). Primary job creation output of the project.

Social & Co-Benefits

👩‍💼
Female economic empowerment

Women's cooperative anchors governance, earns from logistics, heat, and digestate sales. Creates durable economic agency independent of seasonal tourism.

🏝️
Island energy resilience

Reduces dependence on expensive island grid imports. Protects 30 households from volatile electricity prices.

🌱
Natura 2000 protection

Diverts organic waste from illegal dumping and landfill leachate that threatens Gulf of Kalloni wetland. Digestate replaces chemical fertilisers near waterways.
